Bali's roads can feel chaotic at first glance — but there is an underlying rhythm to the traffic that becomes familiar quickly. With a little preparation, navigating the island by scooter or car is one of the most enjoyable parts of the Bali experience.

## Understanding Bali's Traffic

Bali drives on the **left side of the road**, like the rest of Indonesia. Traffic in Kuta, Seminyak, and Canggu can be genuinely gridlocked during peak hours (roughly 7–9am and 4–7pm). Sanur, by contrast, is relatively calm and makes an excellent base for day trips.

The unofficial rule of Balinese traffic is that whoever commits first has right of way — so indicate clearly, move decisively, and stay alert.

## Licences and Legal Requirements

To legally ride a scooter in Bali you need either:
- An **Indonesian driving licence (SIM C)** for motorcycles, or
- A valid **home country licence plus an International Driving Permit (IDP)**

For cars you need a **SIM A** or an IDP endorsed for cars. Spot checks by traffic police do happen, particularly around Kuta and Seminyak. Fines for riding without a licence start around IDR 250,000.

## Road Conditions to Know

- **Potholes** are everywhere — especially on smaller inland roads. Always scan ahead.
- **Speed bumps (polisi tidur)** appear without warning, even on main roads. Slow right down.
- **Dogs** roam freely and dart across roads unpredictably, especially at dawn and dusk.
- **Ceremony traffic:** Temple processions can temporarily close entire roads. Build in extra time during Galungan and Kuningan festivals.

## Parking in Bali

Most attractions have designated parking areas with a small fee (typically IDR 2,000–5,000 for scooters). In towns, look for hand-painted parking signs or follow the flow of other vehicles. Never park blocking a temple gate or ceremony space.

## Fuel

Pertamina petrol stations are widespread and reliable. Pertalite (pink pumps) is the standard unleaded fuel used by most scooters. Pertamax is the higher-octane option recommended for newer bikes like the Yamaha NMAX or Honda PCX.

Avoid the small plastic bottles sold at roadside stalls if you can — the quality is inconsistent and the price is rarely better once you factor in the dilution risk.

## Our Top Safety Tips

1. **Always wear your helmet**, even for short trips.
2. **Avoid riding at night** if you are unfamiliar with the roads.
3. **Keep to quieter roads** until you are comfortable with Bali traffic patterns.
